Objective

My department, Engineering Physics, hosts a Robot Competition every year where teams of four students build autonomous robots to compete in a custom course with objectives and obstacles changing each year. The Robot Competition is the culmination of ENPH 253, an in-depth course in design and fabrication developed with inspiration from both Stanford's ME210 and MIT's ME2.007. It is both ridiculously intensive and rewarding, giving students a plethora of applicable skills. Because of this, it's the only school project which I've included in my portfolio.

For the first half of the summer, students take a packed academic term consisting of frequent ENPH 253 labs and various other coursework. The second half of the summer is dedicated to the Robot Competition, with students often putting in 12 hour days 6 or 7 days a week in the month leading up to the competition.

A practice run of our robot completing the majority of the obstacles present in the course.

Competition Course

The course, Star-Wars themed, had stuffed ewoks at various locations. Points were awarded for "rescuing" the ewoks by picking them up and returning them to the starting area.

There were many obstacles, including:

  • A 6" gap

  • A narrow archway, with an IR beacon indicating when it was safe to pass through

  • A second gap, this time to an elevated, round platform

  • A 1" aluminum tube that robots could use to traverse the gap, if they dared

  • An unmarked suspension bridge

Design Details

As a broad overview, our strategy was:

  • Pick up the first ewok

  • Dump a bridge across the first gap and move to the second platform

  • Pick up the second ewok

  • Traverse the second platform and pick up the third ewok (but keep it in our claw as out basket was too small to hold three)

  • Dump the ewoks over the wall to the starting platform, securing points

  • Ascend the aluminum tube via zipline mechanism

  • Pick up the forth ewok

  • Traverse the bridge, using the edge of the bridge as a guide (by measuring reflectance)

  • Pick up Chewbacca

  • Zipline back to the starting area

This was a very ambitious strategy, but had the added benefit of securing many points early on.

Zipline Mechanism

The zipline mechanism had two components: the extension, and the hook.

The extension was actuated by a carriage on a power screw with limit switches on both ends. I personally designed the hook which sat atop, latched onto the aluminum tube, and pulled the robot across. I've separated that into another post.

Software

I wrote most of the infrastructure that my group built the codebase on. The robot was designed as a Finite State Machine, with many states corresponding to the different tasks necessary to traverse the course. You can view our source code here. However, be warned: we were very stressed for time during the weeks leading up to the competition, and it showed in our code style. View the commit history at your own risk!

Controls

We had QRD sensors to measure the reflectance of the ground in front of our robot. For parts of the course with a black tape path, this allowed us to determine whether or not the robot was on or off the path. By using multiple (four) sensors, we were able to determine an error value which we used in a simple PID algorithm to smoothly follow the tape. This same algorithm was used to have the robot follow the edge of the unmarked suspension bridge, by attaching sensors to its side.

I took on a few tasks regarding off-path navigation. The robot had rotary encoders coupled to the rear wheels which I designed a simple RC debounce circuit for. This allowed us to obtain an accurate measure of how much our wheels had spun. By assigning error to the difference in how many intervals each wheel had spun through, I was able to again use PID to design an algorithm causing the robot to move forwards/backwards in a straight line, or turn in place.

Wheels

We decided on a 2-wheel RWD and passive coasters in front. RWD was chosen over FWD due to the slight advantage in handling, which was useful for the tight maneuvers necessary in the course. We chose a passive coasters as our front "wheels" due to small ridges and gaps present throughout the course; the availabel rigid wheels would often get stuck on these ridges and gaps. Additionally, the coasters were very easy to install and modify.

Power

Competition rules dictated that each group was allowed use of a single 14.8V LiPo and two 7.4V LiPos.

DC Motors were powered by the 14.8V LiPo through custom-built H-bridge boards (off-the-shelf H-bridges were banned). The servos were powered by the same LiPo put through a voltage regulator. Sensor circuits were powered by the 7.4V LiPos.

As course attempts were limited to just 2 minutes, power consumption was not a large concern for this project.

Mistakes

Given the chance, I would have liked to do things very, very differently.

  • On the day before competition day, an extra competition surface was built. This had two new aluminum tubes with a significantly thicker diameter (~3mm), and I had to refabricate the hook within a couple hours. In hindsight, I should have seen this possibility and designed the hook to be more easily adjustable.

  • Before the competition, we re-routed the wiring to isolate the servo circuits from sensor circuits. In doing this, we introduced two problems: the servo input signal was getting distorted, causing inconsistent behaviour for the claw mechanism, and the voltage regulators would blow often. Due to the lack of time, we were unable to fully debug this before the competition and as such lost many points due to inconsistent claws.
    We should have better planned our power circuits ahead of time. This would have saved an enormous amount of time re-wiring and debugging.

  • In general, much of our time was wasted fabricating and testing concepts which I know now were hopeless. It would have been extremely beneficial to do a weighted decision matrix (or similar) to more accurately evaluate concepts before committing to ideas.

The Conclusion

Despite the many setbacks, our robot was able to perform very well. This included:

  • Being the only group in the competition to successfully make use of a zipline mechanism (and hence being an immediate crowd favourite)

  • Being one of several groups to have an 100% IR detection rate

  • Finishing in the top 8 of the competition
